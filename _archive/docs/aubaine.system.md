# Aubaine — System Overview

*A Structural Presentation of the Core System*

Aubaine operates on a unified resolution structure designed to remain consistent across all forms of play. Whether a character attempts a social maneuver, a physical feat, an attack, or resists an imposed effect, the mechanical foundation remains the same. This consistency ensures clarity while preserving flexibility in interpretation.

## Core Resolution

All actions in Aubaine begin in fiction. When uncertainty demands resolution, the Game Master assigns an **Ability** and an **Aptitude** appropriate to the declared action. The player then rolls:

**1d20 + Ability modifier + Aptitude modifier**

The result is compared against a Difficulty Class (DC). For general ability rolls, the DC is determined by the Game Master according to narrative difficulty. For effects created by skills, the standard formula for a Saving DC is:

**8 + relevant Ability modifier + relevant Aptitude modifier**

All saving rolls follow the same structure as other rolls: the defender rolls **1d20 + Ability modifier + Aptitude modifier** against the calculated DC. In all cases of tied results, the defender prevails.

All fractions in Aubaine are rounded up.

## Abilities

Characters possess seven Abilities: Strength, Dexterity, Perception, Constitution, Intelligence, Spirit, and Charisma. Abilities are expressed exclusively as modifiers ranging from **–5 to +5**, with a default value of **0**. Abilities represent the method through which an action is performed — physical force, agility, resilience, reasoning, awareness, conviction, or presence.

Abilities do not restrict what a character may attempt. Instead, they influence how effectively a chosen approach performs.

## Aptitudes

Characters possess twenty-four Aptitudes representing domains of expertise, alongside a unique Signature Aptitude defined by the character. Aptitudes are expressed as modifiers ranging from **–6 to +6**, with a default value of **0**.

If Abilities define how an action is executed, Aptitudes define in which domain the action resides. The pairing of Ability and Aptitude is determined by the fiction of the action rather than predefined associations.

## Luck

Luck is a secondary attribute represented as a score rather than a modifier. It is determined by rolling:

**(3d100 ÷ 3) + 1d20**, with all results rounded up.

Luck Rolls are resolved by rolling **1d100**. If the result is equal to or below the character’s Luck score, the roll succeeds. A result of **1–5** is a critical success. A result of **96–100** is a critical failure. Luck Rolls inherit the critical effects associated with the type of situation they influence.

## Types of Rolls

Aubaine recognizes five categories of rolls, each using the same resolution structure but differing in context.

An **Ability Roll** uses a DC determined by the Game Master.

An **Attack Roll** compares the attacker’s roll against the target’s Armor Class.

A **Saving Roll** is made against a DC generated by a skill’s formula.

A **Luck Roll** uses the Luck system described above.

An **Opposed Roll** requires all involved parties to roll; the highest total succeeds. In the event of a tie, the defender prevails.

## Critical Results

A natural roll of **1** is always a critical failure. It results in automatic failure regardless of modifiers and introduces a negative twist determined by the Game Master.

A natural roll of **20** is always a critical success. It results in automatic success regardless of modifiers and applies an additional effect depending on the roll type. For Ability Rolls, the player introduces a favorable twist consistent with the fiction. For Attack Rolls, damage dice are doubled. For Saving Rolls, no additional effect occurs beyond the automatic success. For Opposed Rolls, a critical success results in automatic victory unless multiple participants roll critical successes, in which case the highest total prevails.

## Turn Structure

Time in Aubaine is structured in six-second turns. During a turn, a character may perform movement, one Action, one Bonus Action, and one Reaction as permitted by circumstances. Skills specify which type of timing they require.

## Core Resources

Characters possess several core resources.

**Hit Points (HP)** represent physical endurance and survivability.

**Energy** is the primary resource consumed by most skills.

**Memory** determines how many Active skills a character may have equipped simultaneously.

**Armor Class (AC)** defines the difficulty of landing successful attacks against the character.

These values are derived from character attributes and progression systems but function universally across all play contexts.

## Rest and Recovery

A **Long Rest** consists of eight hours of rest and restores all primary resources.

Between Long Rests, characters may take up to two **Short Rests**, each lasting ten minutes. A Short Rest restores half of a character’s maximum Hit Points and half of their maximum Energy.

## Experience and Progression

Characters earn Experience Points (XP) through play. XP is used to develop the character according to player intent. It may be invested in increasing Abilities, improving Aptitudes, expanding core resources, or acquiring and developing skills through structured skill trees and shared skill banks.

Progression in Aubaine is player-directed. The system does not impose predefined advancement paths; instead, XP allows characters to grow in the directions chosen by their players. The cost of progression increases as investment deepens, ensuring that specialization and expansion remain deliberate choices.

## Skill Structure

Skills in Aubaine are categorized structurally rather than thematically.

**Active Skills** require timing to use and often consume Energy.

**Passive Skills** provide continuous benefits without direct activation.

**Ultimate Skills** represent powerful, limited-use abilities unique to each character.

**Evolutions** and **Upgrades** modify or enhance existing skills, expanding their capabilities over time.

The structure of skills is consistent across the system, allowing interpretation and customization without altering the underlying resolution mechanics.
