# Aubaine

*A Fiction-First, Progression-Based TTRPG*

Aubaine is built on a simple conviction: imagination comes first. The rules exist to support the fiction, not to confine it. Characters grow, evolve, and gain tangible power over time, but that progression should never make a player feel restricted in what they can attempt. Your sheet does not define the limits of your creativity — it highlights where you shine, where you are exceptional, and where you bend the world more easily than others.

At its heart, Aubaine follows a fiction-first philosophy. You begin by describing what your character does in the story. Only afterward do mechanics step in, if uncertainty demands it. The purpose of resolution is not to decide whether you were “allowed” to try, but to determine the outcome, the cost, and the consequences. Success, complication, sacrifice, and momentum all flow from the narrative first.

Everything in Aubaine rests on three fundamental principles.

The first is the Rule of Cool. If you attempt something bold, cinematic, clever, or dramatically satisfying, you are allowed to try it — even if another rule might suggest otherwise. The game should open doors for style and daring, not close them. The mechanics may influence how well it works or what it costs you, but they should not deny the attempt simply because it was not prewritten on your character sheet. Aubaine rewards imagination and bravery in equal measure.

The second is the Rule of Customization. When you gain a new ability — a skill, spell, technique, feature, or any form of advancement — you may shape it to reflect your character’s identity. A fire spell may become ice. A shield may become a mirror of light. A stealth technique may become shadow-melding or perfect stillness. Customization is encouraged, so long as it preserves balance and respects the spirit of the game. Changes should first serve fiction and expression; mechanical adjustments must remain equivalent in power or be balanced through appropriate cost or risk. Customization is a tool for identity, not optimization abuse.

The third is the Rule of Good Faith. The freedoms granted by Aubaine exist to make the game more engaging for everyone at the table. They are not loopholes to exploit. Players are expected to customize, attempt daring actions, and build powerful abilities with the intention of enriching the shared story — not dominating it. Good Faith means considering tone, spotlight, and fairness. If a choice undermines the collective experience, it contradicts the foundation of the game.

When these principles intersect, Good Faith stands above all. Customization and Cool are powerful tools, but they must serve the shared story. Mechanics support the principles, not the other way around.

Aubaine is a game of growth, audacity, and trust. It asks players to be imaginative, and it asks them to be responsible with that imagination. In return, it promises a system that will rarely say “no,” and instead will ask: “What happens next?”
