# Documented TTRPG Best Practices

This file documents the design practices the skill applies.

Each practice includes the principle, application, and output check.

## 1. Write Situations, Not Plots

**Principle:** A scenario should not depend on players following a scripted sequence.

**Apply:** Create locations, NPC goals, factions, clues, timers, obstacles, and consequences. Let the players choose the route.

**Check:** If one missed scene breaks the adventure, redesign it.

**Source map:** The Alexandrian's node-based scenario design and open-table writing emphasize robust structures over linear dependency.

## 2. Use Redundant Clues

**Principle:** Important conclusions need redundancy.

**Apply:** For each conclusion required for progress, include at least three clues. Place them in different forms and places.

**Check:** A failed roll, missed room, or suspicious player should not halt the mystery.

**Source map:** The Alexandrian's Three Clue Rule; GUMSHOE's core clue logic.

## 3. Put Core Clues in Reach

**Principle:** Investigative play is about interpreting clues, not failing to find them.

**Apply:** If a clue is required, make it available through competent action, not a single pass/fail gate.

**Check:** Failed rolls may add cost, time, danger, or incomplete detail, but do not erase the only path forward.

**Source map:** GUMSHOE and Trail of Cthulhu cheat-sheet guidance on automatic core clues.

## 4. Start Strong

**Principle:** The opening should put the table into motion.

**Apply:** Begin with pressure, a choice, a visible problem, an unstable situation, or a sensory fact that demands action.

**Check:** The first scene should not require long exposition before play starts.

**Source map:** Sly Flourish's strong-start prep advice.

## 5. Prepare Secrets and Clues

**Principle:** Portable revelations make prep flexible.

**Apply:** Write secrets as facts the characters can discover through different routes.

**Check:** A secret should be usable in more than one scene if needed.

**Source map:** Sly Flourish's secrets and clues method.

## 6. Review the Characters

**Principle:** Prep improves when tied to the player characters.

**Apply:** Connect hooks, NPCs, rewards, dilemmas, and threats to character goals, bonds, flaws, factions, and resources.

**Check:** At least one part of the content should matter differently because of who the characters are.

**Source map:** Sly Flourish's Lazy DM steps.

## 7. Make Choices Meaningful

**Principle:** A choice matters when players can compare risk, cost, reward, and consequence.

**Apply:** Show tradeoffs. Offer multiple viable approaches.

**Check:** If all options lead to the same result, cut or change the choice.

## 8. Telegraph Danger

**Principle:** Severe consequences should be foreshadowed.

**Apply:** Use corpses, scorch marks, rumors, tracks, broken tools, changed behavior, warnings, or small versions of the threat.

**Check:** Players should be able to say, “We were warned.”

## 9. Failure Changes the Situation

**Principle:** Failure should move play, not stop it.

**Apply:** Use costs, clocks, exposure, damage, lost resources, bad bargains, delays, or escalation.

**Check:** After a failure, something is different.

**Source map:** PbtA GM moves and FitD consequences/clocks.

## 10. Use Clocks for Complex Pressure

**Principle:** Ongoing threats and layered obstacles need visible tracking.

**Apply:** Use 4-segment clocks for simple complex tasks, 6 for involved pressure, 8+ for major or long-term threats.

**Check:** Name clocks after obstacles or outcomes, not methods. “Guards Alerted” is better than “Sneak Past Guards.”

**Source map:** Blades in the Dark progress clock guidance.

## 11. Define NPCs by Play Function

**Principle:** NPCs need motives and leverage more than biography.

**Apply:** Give each important NPC a want, fear, secret, resource, visible trait, and next action if ignored.

**Check:** The GM should know what the NPC says, wants, offers, hides, and does next.

## 12. Make Factions Active

**Principle:** Factions make the world move when players do not.

**Apply:** Give factions goals, methods, resources, enemies, internal tensions, current actions, and next moves.

**Check:** If ignored, a faction should change the situation.

## 13. Build Encounters Around Objectives

**Principle:** Encounters are not always fights.

**Apply:** Define what each side wants, what changes over time, what can be exploited, and how the encounter can end.

**Check:** Combat, negotiation, stealth, retreat, trickery, and sacrifice should all be considered when appropriate.

## 14. Use Terrain and Constraints

**Principle:** Good tactical scenes have changing conditions and usable space.

**Apply:** Add cover, height, hazards, time limits, unstable objects, bystanders, ritual progress, alarms, weather, darkness, or movement constraints.

**Check:** The map or fictional space should matter.

## 15. Make Monsters Play Differently

**Principle:** A monster should create decisions, not only deal damage.

**Apply:** Give signs, behavior, tactics, weaknesses, counters, and a clear combat or horror role.

**Check:** Players should change tactics once they understand the creature.

## 16. Make Dungeons Jaquayed

**Principle:** Dungeons are stronger when they include loops, alternate routes, connections, and meaningful navigation.

**Apply:** Add visible goals, locked shortcuts, faction territory, verticality, loops, secret doors, hazards, and landmarks.

**Check:** Players should be able to choose routes and use knowledge of the space.

**Source map:** The Alexandrian's dungeon and node-based design essays.

## 17. Track Dungeon Time

**Principle:** Time creates risk and resource decisions.

**Apply:** Use turns, light, noise, wandering encounters, rests, faction movement, or countdowns when exploration matters.

**Check:** Delay should have a cost.

## 18. Use Reaction and Morale Where Appropriate

**Principle:** Not every creature fights to the death.

**Apply:** Give monsters and NPCs instincts, wants, reactions, and morale breakpoints.

**Check:** Parley, retreat, bribery, fear, and surrender should exist when genre-appropriate.

**Source map:** OSR play culture and classic dungeon procedures.

## 19. Support Player Ingenuity

**Principle:** Fictional positioning should matter.

**Apply:** Reward careful questions, equipment use, scouting, negotiation, map knowledge, and clever plans.

**Check:** Do not reduce every problem to a character-sheet button.

**Source map:** OSR principles: rulings over rules, player skill, and meaningful fictional interaction.

## 20. Make Rules Text Procedural

**Principle:** A rule is usable when the reader knows when and how to use it.

**Apply:** Write rules in this order: purpose, trigger, steps, outcomes, limits, example.

**Check:** A new GM should be able to run it without inference.

## 21. Avoid Unnecessary Mechanics

**Principle:** Rules add cognitive load.

**Apply:** Add a mechanic only when it creates choices, costs, pacing, tension, genre feel, or fair resolution.

**Check:** If a rule only restates common sense, cut it.

## 22. Respect Action Economy

**Principle:** In tactical systems, number of turns often matters as much as raw stats.

**Apply:** Consider side count, initiative, control effects, reactions, legendary actions, hazards, minions, and objectives.

**Check:** One boss against many characters needs tools beyond higher numbers.

## 23. Fit the System's Reward Loop

**Principle:** Content should reward what the system is about.

**Apply:** Match incentives to the game: treasure for exploration, XP triggers for beliefs, stress and payoff for heists, clues for investigations, bonds for drama.

**Check:** Rewards should push the intended playstyle.

## 24. Use Player-Facing Information

**Principle:** Players make better choices with clear information.

**Apply:** Provide rumors, maps, visible clocks, known risks, telegraphed threats, NPC demands, and partial truths.

**Check:** The table should not depend on GM secrecy to function.

## 25. Leave Blanks

**Principle:** Overdefined settings can crowd out play.

**Apply:** Define what matters now. Leave space for player contributions, future revelations, and GM improvisation.

**Check:** If lore does not affect current choices, compress it.

## 26. Use Random Tables as Engines

**Principle:** A table should create action, not decoration.

**Apply:** Each entry should add a clue, pressure, opportunity, threat, cost, relationship, or decision.

**Check:** Avoid entries that only say “strange sound” or “odd smell.” Say what they imply or trigger.

## 27. Write Treasure as Decisions

**Principle:** Rewards should change play.

**Apply:** Make treasure useful, costly, cursed, traceable, faction-linked, bulky, time-sensitive, or morally complicated.

**Check:** A reward should create action after it is found.

## 28. Keep Lore Actionable

**Principle:** Lore belongs in play when it changes decisions.

**Apply:** Tie history to clues, faction motives, dangers, routes, rituals, passwords, maps, or NPC behavior.

**Check:** If lore cannot be discovered or acted on, cut or move it to a short GM note.

## 29. Use Safety Tools for Sensitive Content

**Principle:** Player well-being matters more than fictional intensity.

**Apply:** Include content notes, session zero prompts, lines and veils, pause/rewind/fast-forward tools, and opt-out procedures when needed.

**Check:** Sensitive material should be avoidable, adjustable, or replaceable.

**Source map:** TTRPG Safety Toolkit, X-Card, Consent in Gaming.

## 30. Design for Accessibility

**Principle:** Clear design reaches more tables.

**Apply:** Use readable layout, plain language, heading hierarchy, adequate spacing, screen-reader-friendly ordering, and non-color-only distinctions.

**Check:** The text should remain usable when printed in black and white, read aloud, searched, or skimmed mid-session.

**Source map:** Tabletop accessibility guidance and accessible rulebook design commentary.

## 31. Provide Multiple Endings

**Principle:** Adventures should respond to player action.

**Apply:** Write outcomes based on what players save, break, reveal, bargain away, or ignore.

**Check:** Avoid one canonical ending.

## 32. Make Villains Act Offscreen

**Principle:** Threats should not wait passively.

**Apply:** Give villains a countdown, next three moves, resources, escape plan, and visible consequences.

**Check:** If the players spend a day elsewhere, something should happen.

## 33. Use Hooks With Actionable Terms

**Principle:** Hooks should tell players what action is available.

**Apply:** Include patron, reward, danger, deadline, location, and a first lead.

**Check:** A hook should answer “Why go now?”

## 34. Support Improvisation

**Principle:** GMs need durable components, not fragile scripts.

**Apply:** Provide names, motives, clues, rumors, complications, prices, reactions, and consequences.

**Check:** If players skip a scene, the GM can still run the scenario.

## 35. Match Tone With Procedures

**Principle:** Rules create genre.

**Apply:** Horror uses scarcity, uncertainty, vulnerability, and costs. Adventure uses choices, risk, and reward. Intrigue uses leverage, secrets, and consequences. Heists use position, clocks, load, flashbacks, and heat.

**Check:** The mechanics should push the promised tone.
