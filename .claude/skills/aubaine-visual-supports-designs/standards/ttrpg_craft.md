# TTRPG craft standards

## Rules are procedures

A useful rule answers:

- When does this happen?
- Who chooses?
- What do they roll, spend, mark, reveal, or risk?
- What are the possible outcomes?
- What changes in the fiction or game state?
- Where does the group look next?

Rewrite passive rules into active procedures.

Weak:

> Characters may sometimes be affected by fear in dangerous situations.

Stronger:

> When a character sees a horror that violates their belief about the world, the player marks 1 Stress or names what they abandon to stay calm. If Stress reaches 5, the GM asks which instinct takes over: flee, freeze, bargain, or lash out.

## The first-session path

Every substantial TTRPG support should include:

- What this game is about.
- What players need at the table.
- Core mechanic.
- How to make or choose a character.
- How the GM starts the first scene.
- How danger, failure, advancement, and recovery work.
- Safety/consent expectations when content warrants it.

## Adventure/module structure

A table-useful adventure should include:

- Premise in one sentence.
- Stakes and timer.
- Entrance points.
- Faction/NPC motivations.
- Location map/key or node map.
- Situation, not a scripted plot.
- Encounter procedures.
- Rumors/clues.
- Treasure/rewards/costs.
- Escalation table.
- Consequences and exit states.

## Stat blocks

Stat blocks should be consistent and play-facing:

- Name and role.
- What they want.
- What they do first.
- What makes them dangerous.
- What changes when hurt, scared, bribed, exposed, or cornered.
- Mechanical values in stable order.
- Page references for unusual rules.

## Random tables

Random tables should create decisions, not just flavor.

- Include die range.
- Use concrete, actionable entries.
- Include at least one entry that changes the situation.
- Consider escalation or clocks when repeated rolls happen.
- Avoid entries that require the GM to invent the actual usable content.

## Examples of play

Examples must be short and precise:

- Show the trigger.
- Show the player choice.
- Show the roll or procedure.
- Show the consequence.
- Do not turn examples into lore fiction.

## Index and cross-references

TTRPG books benefit from redundancy:

- Glossary for game terms.
- Index for conditions, rules, monsters, spells, tags, subsystems, and tables.
- Page references in stat blocks and tables.
- Appendix with all high-frequency rules.

## Playtest and balance claims

Never claim balance, fairness, extensive playtesting, accessibility compliance, print compliance, or legal compatibility unless the project includes evidence. Use factual language:

- “Prototype rule; needs table test.”
- “Checked with automated contrast script; manual review still required.”
- “Exported using PDF/X-4 preset; printer approval pending.”
