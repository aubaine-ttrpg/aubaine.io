# No-AI-tell editorial standard

This standard means the artifact should read as careful, specific, edited work. It does **not** mean hiding material facts, fabricating credentials, claiming human-only authorship, or inventing playtest/legal/printing approval.

## Banned or suspicious patterns

Flag and usually rewrite:

- “As an AI,” “I cannot,” “generated by,” “in this comprehensive guide,” “delve,” “tapestry,” “embark,” “unleash your imagination,” “richly detailed world,” “immersive experience,” “dynamic and engaging,” “seamlessly blends,” “whether you are a seasoned veteran or newcomer.”
- Smooth paragraphs that say nothing testable.
- Symmetrical bullet lists where every item has the same rhythm but no table function.
- Lore that names vibes but not people, places, constraints, resources, or conflicts.
- Generic fantasy nouns: shadows, echoes, ancient, forgotten, realm, destiny, secrets, whispers, unless made specific.
- Fake specificity: “three factions” followed by three interchangeable factions.
- Faux authority: “best-in-class,” “professionally balanced,” “print ready,” “fully accessible,” unless verified.

## Replacement principles

- Replace mood with situation.
- Replace adjectives with constraints.
- Replace lore paragraphs with table-facing artifacts: rumors, handouts, keyed rooms, NPC moves, clocks, costs, procedures, random tables.
- Replace “players can explore” with concrete choices and consequences.
- Replace “the GM may decide” with criteria, default consequences, and examples.
- Preserve intentional style, but cut filler.

## Human editorial pass

For every section, ask:

- What can the GM/player do with this at the table?
- What is the most concrete noun in this paragraph?
- What choice does this create?
- What happens if the players ignore it?
- Is the voice specific to this game, or could it appear in any fantasy RPG?
- Are citations and credits real?
- Are production claims verified?

## Voice calibration

Build a voice card before drafting:

- Three comparable books/games for tone, not for copying.
- Forbidden words.
- Preferred sentence length.
- Humor level.
- Violence/horror boundaries.
- Address style: direct instructions, second-person, neutral technical, in-world fragments, etc.
- Table language: roll, test, check, move, mark, spend, burn, risk, reveal.

## Mechanical prose rule

When explaining a rule, use this order:

1. Trigger.
2. Actor.
3. Choice.
4. Cost/roll/resource.
5. Outcome bands.
6. Fictional consequence.
7. Example or edge case.
8. Cross-reference.

If the rule cannot fit this order, the design likely needs clarification.
