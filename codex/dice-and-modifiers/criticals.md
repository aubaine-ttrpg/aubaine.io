# Criticals

## What it is

The two ways a roll breaks past ordinary success or failure.

## How it works

- **Critical success**: the three kept dice all show 12. You get the best possible version of
  what you were attempting, plus a perk the GM grants. On an attack, you also double the damage.
- **Critical failure**: the three kept dice all show 1. The GM chooses a bad turn of events.

A critical success does what you were trying to do, at its best. It does not rewrite the scene
into something unrelated.

## Limits

Advantage and Disadvantage shift the odds sharply. Under Advantage a critical success needs
three 12s among the dice you rolled, so it grows likelier, while a critical failure needs every
die to show 1, so it nearly vanishes. Disadvantage mirrors this. The exact rates are computed in
the lab.

## At the table

> **Player:** 3d12: 12, 12, 12.
> **GM:** Critical. The blade goes exactly where you aim, double damage, and the captain reels
> back into the brazier.

## Design Notes

Bounding a critical to the attempted action keeps it a strong result, not a wish granted: a
player who stacks Advantage to fish for 12s gets the best version of what they were doing, not
an arbitrary one. Reading criticals off the kept dice, not the total, is what ties them to
Advantage, the lever that makes them appear.
